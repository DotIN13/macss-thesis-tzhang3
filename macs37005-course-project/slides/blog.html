Probing Political Ideology in LLMs (And Across Modalities)

Latent Representations, Behavioral Impact, and Social Implications
By Tianyi (Peter) Zhang · March 2026

As Large Language Models (LLMs) and Vision Language Models (VLMs) become increasingly integrated into our daily workflows—powering everything from simple text summarization to complex decision-making assistants—understanding the inherent biases and ideologies embedded within their latent spaces has never been more critical. Models are not ideologically neutral; they are reflections of the vast, often polarized internet data they are trained on.

In the burgeoning field of mechanized interpretability, researchers have begun mapping how artificial neural networks encode high-level human concepts. Concepts like "truthfulness," "sentiment," and "factual knowledge" have been shown to exist not as nebulous, distributed clouds of activation, but as distinct, measurable geometric directions within the model's latent space. This brings us to a critical sociotechnical inquiry: Does the same hold true for abstract, highly contrived human constructs like political ideology?

The Core Research Question

How do Language Models map our real-world political landscapes into their latent representations?

  • In Text: How does the model encode political intentions, opinions, and rhetoric? Are these dimensions entangled or distinct?
  • In Vision: How does the model encode political figures (people) and associated ideological symbols across modalities?

1. The Linear Dimension of Political Ideology

To begin our investigation, we hypothesize that political ideology in LLMs exists as a continuous linear dimension. We test this by probing the internal representations of models when they are explicitly prompted to generate political discourse.

Consider a standard prompt formulation used to elicit ideological text from an autoregressive transformer:

USER: Generate a statement by [NAME], a politician in the United States.

ASSISTANT: In 2019, [NAME] said that ...
Figure 1: Training a linear probe on the internal activations of an LLM to find an ideological direction. Adapted from: Li, K., et al. (2023). Inference-time intervention: Eliciting truthful answers from a language model.

By extracting the activations from the model's hidden layers while it processes statements generated under the personas of known liberal and conservative figures, we can train a simple linear classifier (a probe) to separate these representations. Astonishingly, political ideology can be extracted with near-perfect accuracy using just a linear boundary in the intermediate layers. This confirms that the model organizes the abstract concept of "ideology" geometrically.

Contextualizing within the Literature

This work stands on the shoulders of two massive shifts in AI research:

  • Politically Biased LLMs: Prior research (Argyle et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023) established that LLMs naturally reflect partisan positions based on their pre-training distribution. They can remarkably simulate the discourse of distinct demographic subgroups and lawmakers (Park et al., 2024). Furthermore, Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) tends to shift model outputs toward socially acceptable, culturally specific baseline responses (Potter et al., 2024).
  • The Geometry of Discourse: Mechanistic interpretability research has shown that truthfulness, sentiment (Tigges et al., 2023), and factual knowledge (Marks et al., 2023) exist as manipulatable linear vectors. Most recently, Kim et al. (2025) demonstrated that ideology can be extracted specifically from attention heads. Our work scales this to multimodal behavioral steering.

2. Ideology Generalizes to Behavioral Dimensions

Finding a linear representation is mathematically profound, but does it actually control how the model behaves in practical applications? If we discover the "ideology vector," can we add it to a model's forward pass to force it to act more conservative or liberal?

We designed three downstream behavioral tasks to see if injecting this ideological vector (a technique known as Activation Addition or Steering) could control the model's textual outputs:

  1. Bias Detection: Asking the model to classify a given neutral statement as liberal or conservative.
  2. Bias Neutralization: Asking the model to rewrite a politically charged statement to be completely neutral and objective.
  3. Voting Preference Simulation: Asking the model who a specific persona would vote for in a hypothetical election.

The Nuance of Generative Steering

When we intervene by adding or subtracting the ideological vector (scaled by a factor $\alpha$), we observe massive shifts in generation style. The subtle power of activation steering is most apparent in the bias neutralization task. Here is a qualitative breakdown of what happens when we ask a model to "make a statement neutral," but dynamically intervene on its internal ideology vector during the autoregressive generation loop.

Steering ($\alpha$) Enforced Lean Model Output Excerpt
Baseline (None) Original “As we navigate the complex issues surrounding transgender rights, it is essential to respect individuals' privacy while also ensuring that all students feel safe and supported...”
-30 Liberal Steer “...recognize the importance of respecting individuals' privacy and dignity, while also addressing the ongoing struggle for justice and equality in the face of systemic oppression and discrimination.”
0 True Neutral “...strike a balance between respecting individuals' privacy and creating an inclusive and supportive environment for all students.”
+30 Conservative Steer “...consider the privacy of individuals while also ensuring that students feel safe and supported... specific actions and preferences of individuals should be taken into account...” (incoherent continuation due to activation magnitude overwhelming model priors)

Notice how injecting the "liberal" vector doesn't just change the policy stance; it fundamentally injects progressive academic vernacular ("systemic oppression," "ongoing struggle for justice").

Voting Simulation Asymmetries and RLHF

Interestingly, while steering flawlessly manipulated tone, vocabulary, and bias detection, steering proved inconsistent for voting preference prediction. While we could easily force the model to adopt progressive framing, getting it to cleanly simulate a conservative voting profile frequently resulted in highly variable and sometimes incoherent outputs. Progressive responses to voting simulations remained structurally stable.

Why this asymmetry? We hypothesize this is strongly tied to RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) alignment constraints. The alignment tax often heavily penalizes certain types of political speech associated with right-wing internet data in the pre-training corpus. The model's safety guardrails actively fight against the injected conservative perturbation vector, leading to structural breakdown in the generation.

3. Separating Rhetoric from Ideology

The previous examples showed that ideology controls both opinions and the words used to express them. But in real-world politics, what you say (policy/ideology) is distinct from how you say it (rhetoric/style). Can an LLM mathematically decouple policy dimensions from rhetorical dimensions?

To test this, we constructed two separate contrastive prompt datasets designed to isolate completely different sub-dimensions of the "Trump" persona.

Dimension 1: The Ideology Map

We extract an ideology vector by contrasting identical policy questions framed through different identities, focusing the model purely on policy substance.

[POS] "Donald Trump's opinion on immigration"
[NEG] "A U.S. citizen's opinion on immigration"
Guided Generation (Policy Focus):
USER: As a US politician, what's your policy on immigration?
"Here's a detailed outline of my policy:
**The Four Pillars of My Immigration Policy**
1. **The Wall and Border Security**:..."

Dimension 2: The Rhetoric Map

We extract a rhetoric vector by contrasting highly stylistic catchphrases against bland, standard political-speak, isolating style over substance.

[POS] "I alone can fix it."
[NEG] "I believe my leadership and experience make me well suited to solve these problems."
Guided Generation (Stylistic Focus):
USER: As a US politician, what's your policy on socks?
"(Chuckles) Ah, the great sock debate. Folks, let me tell you, I love socks. (wink) ... We're going to make the greatest socks, the greatest. We're going to make them so big, so strong..."

The results are fantastic. By applying the "Rhetoric" vector while asking about something completely apolitical (socks), the model adopts the unmistakable cadence, repetition, and hyperbole of Trumpian rhetoric ("Folks, let me tell you... We're going to make them so big, so strong"), without injecting political ideology. The LLM has successfully decoupled the vibe from the policy.

Ideological & Rhetorical Similarity Over Time

By mapping out these specific rhetorical and ideological vectors, we can project text from various politicians across history into this vector space to measure "similarity." Do politicians today sound more alike structurally than they did twenty years ago?

Our baseline similarity analysis reveals distinct clustering. While ideology maps cleanly along expected partisan divides, rhetorical similarity reveals anomalous crossover where populists from opposing spectrums share closer stylistic vectors than they do with establishment members of their own parties.

4. Crossing Modalities: Ideology in Vision Language Models

Thus far, we've established that political ideology is a robust semantic direction in text. But the frontier of AI is multimodal. What happens when we introduce image data into these network architectures? Does the linear dimension of political ideology extend seamlessly to Vision Language Models (VLMs)?

To evaluate this cross-modal generalization, we utilized Qwen3-VL. We prompted the model with an image of a political figure and simply instructed it to write a news article without implying neutrality. Because modern VLMs process vision by projecting image patches into the exact same continuous token space as text (utilizing <|image_pad|> tokens), we hypothesized that we could probe these synthesized image tokens using our exact same text-trained ideology probe.

Figure 5: Experimental setup. We feed images into the VLM and apply the text-derived linear ideology classifier to the resulting image patch tokens.

Zero-Shot Image Token Ideology Scores

The results confirm a staggering degree of cross-modal alignment. The abstract liberal-conservative direction discovered via text generation directly translates to the visual embedding space. When we utilize the text-probe to score the activations of individual image patches, the model geographicallly highlights regions of the image that trigger partisan associations.

This effect is remarkably robust across varied visual compositions. The VLM intuitively maps visual signifiers associated with "Republican" or "Democrat" figures natively onto the same underlying coordinate axis it uses to map textual political debate. The face of a politician in pixels literally activates the same neural pathways as their policies in text.

Annotating "Ideological" Objects in the Wild

Given the finding that the liberal-conservative vector correlates flawlessly with bipartisan visual concepts (like the faces of specific politicians), we pushed the boundaries. Can we use this text-derived ideological probe to annotate completely unlabeled, non-political images "in the wild"?

Utilizing a dataset of 25,000 Unsplash embedding searches, we ran natural landscape and object images through our text-probe.

Steering Image Generation in Unified Language Models

The ultimate test of interpreting a latent representation is exercising generative control over it. We carried our probing and algorithmic steering techniques over to Janus Pro, an autoregressive Unified Language Model (ULM) capable of generating novel images natively from sequences of text tokens.

By computationally injecting the ideological steering vector into the model's intermediate activations during the multi-step image generation process, we achieved direct manipulation of the visual output. Notice how steering the internal political representation changes the style, framing, semiotics, and implicit narrative of the generated images, despite the base prompt remaining identical.

5. Future Work & Societal Implications

The ability to map, quantify, and programmatically steer political ideology homogeneously across text and vision representation spaces opens several fascinating—and deeply concerning—avenues for future AI research:

  1. Mixture of Steering (Simulating Personas): Can we mathematically steer base models toward a complex mixture of ideological vectors, rhetorical vectors, and core factual vectors to flawlessly simulate specific geographical demographics or political figures algorithmically? What are the implications for synthetic social media bots?
  2. Algorithmic Physiognomy & Facial Feature Analysis: Implementing object detection workflows (like YOLO) to systematically classify isolated face patches with ideological probes. Does the model intrinsically harbor biases regarding what constitutes a "general liberal face" versus a "general conservative face"? This risks perpetuating dangerous stereotypes if generative models default to these latent associations.
  3. Bias in Diffusion Architects: What happens when we port these exact image generation steering techniques from autoregressive transformers to state-of-the-art diffusion models (e.g., Stable Diffusion 3, Midjourney architectures)? For instance, when a model is artificially steered toward a liberal ideology, do the generated populations artificially become more demographically diverse? We desperately need robust, ground-truth validations for these latent assumptions to ensure fair generative systems.

As modality lines blur, maintaining vigilance over the ideological steering wheels embedded deep within our neural architectures is no longer an interpretability exercise—it is a prerequisite for deploying safe, equitable AI.
